# Woven vs Printed Labels: Which One for Your Clothing Brand

Your first production run is booked, the order form is open, and there's a box that says "woven" or "printed." Most new founders treat that as one decision for the whole garment. That's the mistake. The neck label and the care label are two different jobs, and they usually want two different methods.

So before you tick one box for everything, it helps to know what each label actually is and where it earns its place.

## The difference in one minute

A woven label is built, not printed. The logo and text are woven from dyed thread on a loom, so the colour is the thread itself. That's why a good woven label never fades — there's no ink to wear off. It also has a textured, slightly raised feel that reads as premium the second a customer touches it.

A printed label is the opposite. Artwork is inked onto a smooth tape — satin, cotton, or polyester — so it can reproduce fine type, gradients, and small multi-line text that a loom can't manage cleanly. Printed satin feels silky and flat. Woven feels like quality. Printed shows detail. Hold both and you can tell in a second.

## This isn't one choice — neck vs care

Here's the part the generic guides skip. You're not picking woven OR printed for the garment. You're picking one for the neck label and one for the care label, because they do different work.

The neck label is the one the customer sees and feels every time they pull the shirt on. It carries your brand. Order it woven. The premium hand-feel and the fact that it won't fade are exactly what protect brand perception — and a scratchy, cheap-feeling neck label is a quiet driver of returns. For a streetwear or boutique brand, this is the label worth spending on.

The care label is a different animal. It has to carry fiber content percentages, wash symbols, and country of origin — often four or five lines of small text. Print that. Woven simply can't render that much fine information legibly at care-label size. So the honest default for most clothing brands is woven at the neck, printed for care. One garment, two methods.

## Cost, MOQ, and the per-piece math

Founders usually reach for "all printed, it's cheaper" — and at low quantity, that instinct isn't wrong. Printed runs roughly 15 to 30 percent cheaper than the same-size woven label when you're ordering small. Woven carries a setup cost for the loom programming, and at 100 or 200 pieces that setup is a big share of the bill.

## What actually survives the wash

Durability is where woven earns its reputation, so let's be honest about both sides. A woven label survives the life of the garment, including a hot industrial wash at a Tiruppur export unit. The thread is dyed; there's nothing to crack. That's why workwear, uniforms, and kidswear that get washed hard usually go woven all the way through.

Printed labels hold up well too, but ink is ink. After 50-odd hot washes, especially on satin, print can crack or fade, and care text can go unreadable inside a year of heavy use. For a fashion piece that's washed gently a few times a season, that's fine. For a garment that gets punished in the laundry, and where the care info legally has to stay readable, it's a real trade-off — either commit to a durable print or accept a woven content label and keep its text short.

## So which do you order?

The rule is short. Default to a woven neck label and a printed care label — that covers most clothing brands. Go all-woven when it's workwear, uniforms, or kidswear washed hard and the care info is short enough to weave. Go all-printed only when budget is tight and the label is a hidden care tag nobody feels.
