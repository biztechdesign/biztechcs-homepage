## The Differentiation Angle

Every top-ranking page on this keyword (Dutch Label Shop, Weavabel, Sienna Pacific, RapidTags, a dozen near-identical "ultimate guide" posts) frames the choice as a single global verdict: "woven is premium and durable, printed is cheaper and detailed, here's a feature table — pick one." Two problems with that. First, none of them are written for an Indian brand: no rupee pricing, no realistic MOQ (they quote 500–10,000 piece minimums or none at all), no mention of what survives a hot Tiruppur-style industrial wash versus a home machine. Second, the framing is wrong — it treats this as one decision when it's really a per-location decision. A clothing brand does not pick woven OR printed for the whole garment; it picks woven for the neck/brand label (where feel and premium perception drive returns and repeat purchase) and printed for the care/content label (where you need four lines of fiber content and wash symbols at a size woven can't render cleanly). Labelwala's version gives the founder a decision framework by label job, with real Indian MOQ (100 pieces) and per-piece numbers (~₹0.25–₹4), so they stop asking "which is better" and start asking "which goes where." That matters to a new brand because getting it wrong means either a scratchy printed neck label that feels cheap against the skin, or an unreadable woven care label that fails compliance and frustrates the customer.

### H2 Sections:

**1. The difference in one minute (woven is built, printed is inked)**
What this section must accomplish: Give the founder the physical/technical distinction fast so the rest of the article makes sense — woven = design built from dyed thread on a loom; printed = artwork inked onto satin/cotton/poly tape.
Key points to cover: Woven is yarn-dyed (colour is the thread, won't fade); printed ink sits on the surface. Woven has a textured premium hand-feel; printed satin is smooth and silky. Woven limited on fine detail and colour count; printed reproduces gradients, small type, photo-like artwork.

**2. Why this isn't one choice — neck label vs care label**
What this section must accomplish: This is the core reframe. Establish that the brand label and the care label are two different jobs and usually want two different methods.
Key points to cover: Neck/brand label = the one the customer sees and feels → woven, because premium feel and zero fade protect brand perception and reduce returns. Care/content label = fiber content %, wash symbols, country of origin, often 3–5 lines small → printed, because woven can't render that much fine text cleanly at care-label size. State the position plainly.

**3. Cost, MOQ and the per-piece math (Indian numbers)**
What this section must accomplish: Replace vague "woven costs more" with real Indian figures and the amortization point a founder needs.
Key points to cover: Printed runs roughly 15–30% cheaper than woven at the same size and low quantity, but woven's setup cost spreads out as volume rises so the gap narrows by ~500–1000 pieces. Labelwala MOQ is 100 (free woven sample kit); per-piece roughly ₹0.25–₹4 by material/method. Most new brands order at the lowest MOQ then re-order three weeks later and pay setup twice — plan the run.

**4. Durability: what actually survives the wash**
What this section must accomplish: Give the honest durability verdict without overstating it, tied to Indian wash conditions.
Key points to cover: Woven survives the life of the garment, including hot industrial wash at an export unit. Printed ink can crack or fade after ~50+ hot washes, especially on satin — care text can go unreadable inside a year of heavy use. So if the care info legally must stay readable, that argues for either a durable print or a woven content label despite the text limits — name the trade-off, don't pretend printed never fades.

**5. So which do you order? (the quick decision rule)** *(if needed)*
What this section must accomplish: Collapse everything into a rule the founder can act on at the order form today.
Key points to cover: Default: woven neck label + printed care label. Go all-woven if it's workwear/uniform/kidswear that gets washed hard and the care info is short. Go all-printed only if budget is tight and the label is a hidden care tag no one feels. Point to ordering a sample kit before committing the full run.
